\noindent\textbf{Executive Summary}\vspace{-0.35em}

\textbf{Strategy.} Momentum is the most reliable equity anomaly in history, but rare
\emph{crash states} wipe out a decade of compounding in weeks. \fundname{} cuts exposure
during these states using an ensemble of three regime signals: a predictive regression (50
percent weight), a gradient-boosted crash classifier (25 percent), and an unsupervised hidden
Markov regime detector (25 percent).

\textbf{Why it beats alternatives.} Static UMD had Sharpe 0.53 with three 40+ percent crashes.
The Daniel and Moskowitz (2015) benchmark (DM) lifted this to 0.74. \fundname{} reaches 0.80
at three-quarters the volatility and two-thirds the drawdown, distinguishable from DM at 5
percent (paired bootstrap $p = 0.041$).

\textbf{Alpha, not compensation for risk.} Monthly alpha against Fama-French five-factor plus
UMD (HAC) is 0.33 percent, or 4.0 percent annualized ($t = 2.63$). All factor betas are below
0.10 and $R^2$ is 0.4 percent: the edge is pure alpha, not a factor tilt. Economic source:
behavioral mispricing combined with conditional management of momentum's structural crash
mechanism.

\textbf{Costs and main risks.} Monthly turnover around 60 percent, under 1.5 percent annual
drag at 20 basis points. Principal risks: a regime shift breaking all three models at once,
short-leg capacity limits on UMD at scale, and finite out-of-sample evidence despite 66 years.

\section{The Momentum Premium and Why It Crashes}

The equity momentum effect (Jegadeesh and Titman 1993) has delivered 7.5 percent annualized
mean return at 14.2 percent volatility since 1960, a Sharpe ratio of 0.53, and is one of the
most robust pricing anomalies on record. Its distribution, however, is far from Gaussian:
excess kurtosis of 10.0, skewness of $-1.3$, and peak drawdowns of 78 percent in 1932 and 58
percent in the 2009 recovery (Figures~\ref{fig:staticumd} and \ref{fig:umddd}).

\begin{figure}[H]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.90\textwidth]{figures/01_umd_cumret_log.png}
\caption{Cumulative return of static UMD, 1927 through 2026, log scale.}
\label{fig:staticumd}
\end{figure}

\begin{figure}[H]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.90\textwidth]{figures/02_umd_drawdown.png}
\caption{Static UMD drawdown from trailing peak, 1927 through 2026.}
\label{fig:umddd}

The mechanism is structural. In extended bear markets, the short leg of momentum concentrates
in high-beta distressed stocks; when markets rebound, those names appreciate several-fold and
inflict catastrophic losses on the short leg. Crashes are therefore not random but conditional
on prior market state, which is what makes them forecastable.

\section{Academic Benchmark: Daniel and Moskowitz (2015)}

Daniel and Moskowitz (2015), denoted DM, show that the conditional mean and variance of UMD
are jointly forecastable from two slow-moving state variables: a bear-market indicator
$I^{B}_t$ equal to one when the cumulative 24-month market excess return is non-positive, and
the six-month realized variance of UMD, $\sigma^2_t$. Their predictive regression is
\begin{equation}
r^{UMD}_{t+1} = \alpha + \beta_1 I^{B}_t + \beta_2 (I^{B}_t \cdot \sigma^2_t) + \beta_3 \sigma^2_t + \varepsilon_{t+1}.
\label{eq:dmreg}
\end{equation}
The $\beta_2$ coefficient is strongly negative: bear markets combined with elevated UMD
variance predict a crash. DM then apply the time-varying mean-variance weight from the
capital allocation literature,
\begin{equation}
w^{DM}_t = c \cdot \frac{\widehat{E}_t[r^{UMD}_{t+1}]}{\widehat{\mathrm{Var}}_t[r^{UMD}_{t+1}]},
\label{eq:dmw}
\end{equation}
where $c$ is chosen to target a fixed unconditional volatility. Our implementation uses EWMA
variance with a six-month half-life (floored at the tenth percentile to prevent leverage
explosions), with the weight clipped to $[-1, +3]$ as a realistic mandate. Under strict
out-of-sample discipline (expanding window, annual refits, first OOS month January 1960,
793 months of forward-tested experience through 2026), DM delivers Sharpe 0.74 with 95 percent
bootstrap CI $[0.47, 0.97]$ and max drawdown 34 percent, materially better than static's 0.53
and 58 percent (Figure~\ref{fig:oosdm}). DM is the benchmark to beat.

\section{Three Orthogonal Timing Approaches}

Can we beat DM? We test three methodologically orthogonal regime detectors, each sharing the
same OOS discipline but using fundamentally different statistical machinery. Each is a
legitimate standalone strategy; the research question is whether their errors are decorrelated
enough to let an ensemble dominate.

\subsection{Approach A: Ridge-regularized linear extension}

Keep DM's framework in (\ref{eq:dmreg}); expand the feature set. We add six-month market
realized volatility, market drawdown, UMD drawdown, and lagged one-month and twelve-month
market returns (eight features total), standardize within each training window, and fit
ridge regression with a five-fold cross-validated L2 penalty. \textbf{Result:} Sharpe 0.69
(CI $[0.41, 0.92]$). L2 shrinkage attenuates DM's crucial bear-times-variance interaction,
and the additional linear features do not compensate.

\subsection{Approach B: Crash-probability classifier with binary shield}

Reframe as classification. Within each training window, label months as crashes if
$r^{UMD}_{t+1}$ falls below the tenth percentile, and fit a gradient-boosted classifier
(200 trees, depth 3, learning rate 0.05). The weight rule is a binary shield:
$w^B_t = 1$ if $\hat{p}^{\text{crash}}_t < 0.05$, else $0$. The threshold is half the training
baseline rate, set a priori. \textbf{Result:} Sharpe 0.63 at 8.2 percent volatility, 24 percent
max drawdown, and positive skew ($+0.43$). Figure~\ref{fig:pcrash} shows the crash probability.

\begin{figure}[H]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.90\textwidth]{figures/13_approach_b_pcrash_timeseries.png}
\caption{Approach B filtered crash probability, 1960 through 2026. Shield active above the
0.05 threshold.}
\label{fig:pcrash}
\end{figure}

\subsection{Approach C: Unsupervised two-state hidden Markov model}

Fit a two-state Gaussian HMM on three observables (market return, market volatility, UMD
volatility). No supervised labels: regimes emerge from the covariance structure. We label the
higher-mean state as \emph{normal} and use the filtered posterior
$P(\text{normal}_t \mid \text{obs}_{1..t})$ from the forward algorithm as the weight.
\textbf{Result:} Sharpe 0.72 (CI $[0.46, 0.96]$), essentially tying DM without any supervised
labels or hand-crafted features (Figure~\ref{fig:pnormal}).

\begin{figure}[H]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.90\textwidth]{figures/16_approach_c_pnormal_timeseries.png}
\caption{Approach C filtered probability of the normal regime, 1960 through 2026.}
\label{fig:pnormal}
\end{figure}

\section{The \fundname{} Ensemble}

None of A, B, C individually beats DM, but their errors are decorrelated: A shrinks the
interaction, B's binary cliff is flat whenever uncertain, C lags regime shifts by a month.
\fundname{} averages them:
\begin{equation}
w^{\fundname}_t = \mathrm{clip}_{[-1, +3]}\!\left( 0.5 \cdot w^{DM}_t + 0.25 \cdot \mathbb{1}\!\{\hat{p}^{\text{crash}}_t < 0.05\} + 0.25 \cdot P(\text{normal}_t) \right).
\label{eq:ensemble}
\end{equation}
The $0.5/0.25/0.25$ weighting is robust: equal-thirds and $0.4/0.3/0.3$ alternatives both
deliver Sharpes of 0.80. Figure~\ref{fig:inddd} shows that \fundname{}'s drawdown is
materially smaller than DM's or static UMD's.

\section{Why It Works and Principal Risks}

The momentum premium reflects slow information diffusion (a behavioral mispricing). Crashes
are conditional, not random: the short leg concentrates in high-beta distressed names during
bear markets and rebounds violently on recovery. DM showed this fragility is forecastable;
\fundname{} extracts additional predictive content through methodologically diverse signals
whose errors cancel on average. The edge persists because shorting UMD is capacity-constrained
at institutional scale, practitioner momentum funds are long-biased and benchmark to static
UMD, and the regime-timing literature is recent. Table~\ref{tab:risks} summarizes the main
risks.

\section{Conclusion}

\fundname{} ensembles three regime-timing signals (DM regression, gradient-boosted crash
classifier, unsupervised HMM) to deliver Sharpe 0.80, 11 percent volatility, and a 22 percent
maximum drawdown over the 1960 through 2026 out-of-sample window. The improvement over the
DM benchmark is statistically significant at the 5 percent level. The edge is pure alpha
(4.0 percent annualized), not a factor tilt, and is robust across decades, cost assumptions,
and ensemble weightings.
