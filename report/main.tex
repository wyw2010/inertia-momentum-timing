\noindent\textbf{\large Executive Summary}
\vspace{-0.2em}

\textbf{Strategy.} Momentum is the most reliable equity anomaly in history, but rare
\emph{crash states} wipe out a decade of compounding in weeks. \fundname{} diagnoses those
states in real time and scales momentum exposure down (or short) during them, using an
ensemble of three regime signals: a predictive regression, a gradient-boosted crash
classifier, and an unsupervised hidden Markov regime detector. Majority ensemble weight (50
percent) sits on the regression; 25 percent on each overlay.

\textbf{Why it beats alternatives.} Static UMD earned a long-run Sharpe of 0.53 but suffered
three crashes exceeding 40 percent. The Daniel and Moskowitz (2015) benchmark (DM) improved
this to 0.74 at the same volatility. \fundname{} reaches 0.80 at three-quarters the volatility
and two-thirds the drawdown. A paired block-bootstrap on the Sharpe difference rejects equality
with DM at the 5 percent level ($p = 0.041$).

\textbf{Alpha, not compensation for risk.} Regressed on the Fama-French five-factor model plus
UMD with HAC standard errors, monthly alpha is 0.33 percent (4.0 percent annualized, $t =
2.63$). Factor betas are all below 0.10 in absolute value and the regression $R^2$ is 0.4
percent. The edge is a behavioral mispricing (slow information diffusion) combined with
conditional risk management at the predictable moments when the momentum short-leg concentrates
in distressed high-beta names that are about to rebound.

\textbf{Costs and main risks.} Turnover is roughly 60 percent monthly, implying under 1.5
percent annual cost drag at 20 basis points per one-way trade. The three principal risks are
(i) a structural regime shift that breaks all three models at once, (ii) short-leg capacity
limits on UMD at institutional scale, and (iii) finite out-of-sample experience despite the
66-year window.

\section{The Momentum Premium and Why It Crashes}

The empirical momentum effect in equity markets was first systematically documented by
Jegadeesh and Titman (1993). A portfolio that is long the stocks that have outperformed over
the prior year and short those that have underperformed earns persistent excess returns over
the subsequent month. The Ken French up-minus-down (UMD) factor, which is our benchmark
momentum portfolio throughout this paper, has delivered an annualized mean return of 7.5
percent at 14.2 percent volatility from 1960 through 2026, a Sharpe ratio of 0.53. Its
performance is not confined to U.S. equities or to any particular decade, and its persistence
is one of the reasons it is regarded as one of the most robust pricing anomalies in the
empirical asset pricing literature.

Yet the distribution of monthly UMD returns is strongly non-Gaussian. Over the 1960 to 2026
window the excess kurtosis is 10.0 and the skewness is negative 1.3. These are fat tails
concentrated on the left. Figure~\ref{fig:staticumd} plots the cumulative return of static
UMD on a logarithmic scale. The long run-up is genuine, but it is interrupted by multi-year
stretches of flat or declining performance. Figure~\ref{fig:umddd} plots the drawdown from
the trailing peak, which makes the cost of the tail risk more visible. Peak drawdown reaches
78 percent in the 1932 aftermath of the Depression and 58 percent in the recovery from the
2008 global financial crisis.

The economic mechanism behind these crashes is now reasonably well understood. During
extended bear markets, the short leg of momentum becomes concentrated in high-beta stocks
that have already suffered substantial price declines. When the market rebounds, these
distressed stocks often appreciate by several multiples, generating catastrophic losses on
the short leg at precisely the moment when the long leg has little upside room left. Momentum
crashes are therefore not random. They are structural, triggered by mean-reversion in
severely depressed stocks conditional on a prior bear market. The question our research
addresses is whether this conditional predictability can be exploited in real time.

\section{The Academic Benchmark: Daniel and Moskowitz (2015)}

The leading academic response to the momentum crash phenomenon is Daniel and Moskowitz (2015),
which we denote DM hereafter. Their central insight is that the conditional mean and
conditional variance of UMD are jointly forecastable from two slowly moving state variables:
a bear-market indicator $I^{B}_t$, equal to one if the cumulative 24-month market excess
return is non-positive, and the realized variance of UMD over the preceding six months,
denoted $\sigma^2_{t}$. Their predictive regression is
\begin{equation}
r^{UMD}_{t+1} \;=\; \alpha \;+\; \beta_1\, I^{B}_t \;+\; \beta_2 \,\bigl(I^{B}_t \cdot \sigma^2_{t}\bigr) \;+\; \beta_3\, \sigma^2_{t} \;+\; \varepsilon_{t+1}.
\label{eq:dmreg}
\end{equation}
The estimated coefficient $\beta_2$ on the interaction term is strongly negative and
statistically significant. The economic interpretation is that when markets have been falling
for two years and momentum itself is running hot, expected UMD returns collapse. This is the
diagnostic fingerprint of a pending crash.

DM then construct a dynamic strategy using the textbook time-varying mean-variance weight
from the capital allocation literature,
\begin{equation}
w^{DM}_t \;=\; c \cdot \frac{\widehat{E}_t[r^{UMD}_{t+1}]}{\widehat{\mathrm{Var}}_t[r^{UMD}_{t+1}]},
\label{eq:dmw}
\end{equation}
where the risk-aversion scaling $c$ is chosen to target a fixed unconditional volatility
comparable to static UMD. In our implementation we use an exponentially weighted moving
average (EWMA) of UMD variance with a half-life of six months as $\widehat{\mathrm{Var}}_t$,
floor it at the tenth percentile of its distribution to prevent leverage explosions in calm
periods, and clip the final weight to $[-1,\,+3]$ to enforce a realistic fund mandate. Our
in-sample replication of the DM strategy roughly doubles the Sharpe ratio from 0.43 to 0.82
and cuts the maximum drawdown from 78 percent to 34 percent, consistent with their
published results.

Before adopting the DM result as our benchmark, however, we submit it to genuine
out-of-sample discipline. We refit the predictive regression annually on an expanding
training window starting in 1928. The earliest out-of-sample month is January 1960, giving
us 793 months of forward-tested experience through February 2026. Over this window DM still
outperforms static UMD meaningfully: Sharpe 0.74 against 0.53, with a 95 percent
block-bootstrap confidence interval of $[0.47,\,0.97]$. The maximum drawdown falls to 34
percent, skewness from $-1.3$ to $-0.3$, and excess kurtosis from 10.0 to 4.4.
Figure~\ref{fig:oosdm} shows the OOS comparison. The DM benchmark is a high bar that an
honest timing strategy must clear.

\section{Three Orthogonal Timing Approaches}

Our research question is whether DM's predictive regression is already the statistical ceiling
on what can be learned about momentum crashes, or whether alternative machinery extracts
additional signal. We test three approaches that share the same out-of-sample discipline and
the same feature environment but differ fundamentally in how they generate regime signals.
Each approach, used alone, constitutes a legitimate standalone strategy. The interesting
question is whether their errors are uncorrelated enough that an ensemble dominates any
individual approach.

\subsection{Approach A: An expanded linear model with ridge regularization}

The most conservative extension of DM keeps the predictive regression framework in equation
(\ref{eq:dmreg}) and expands the feature set. In addition to the DM originals, we add the
six-month market realized volatility, the current market drawdown from its trailing peak, the
UMD drawdown, and lagged one-month and twelve-month market excess returns. We standardize the
eight features within each training window, then fit a ridge regression where the L2 penalty
is chosen by five-fold cross-validation on a log-spaced grid. Five-fold rather than
leave-one-out cross-validation matters: leave-one-out is notoriously noisy in the small
samples we encounter at the start of each refit window and selects severely over-regularized
models.

The out-of-sample Sharpe ratio is 0.69 with 95 percent bootstrap confidence interval
$[0.41,\,0.92]$. The paired Sharpe difference relative to DM is slightly negative at $-0.05$,
and its confidence interval contains zero. The honest finding is that expanding a linear
model does not beat DM's three-variable specification. The culprit is shrinkage. The ridge
penalty attenuates DM's critical bear-times-variance interaction, which DM rely on heavily,
without extracting enough signal from the new features to compensate. This is consistent with
the broader lesson from the machine learning literature in finance that regularized linear
models often tie simpler specifications in low signal-to-noise regimes.

\subsection{Approach B: A crash-probability classifier with a binary shield}

Our second approach reframes the problem entirely. Rather than forecasting expected returns,
we forecast the probability that next month's UMD return falls below the tenth percentile of
the in-sample UMD distribution. Within each training window we compute this tenth-percentile
threshold, label each month accordingly, and fit a gradient-boosted classifier with fixed
hyperparameters (200 trees of maximum depth three, learning rate 0.05, row subsampling 0.8).
The classifier outputs a filtered crash probability $\hat{p}^{\text{crash}}_t$ for each
out-of-sample month.

The weight rule is a binary crash shield. We take full momentum exposure when the classifier
is confident that crash conditions are absent and zero exposure otherwise:
\begin{equation}
w^{B}_t \;=\; \begin{cases} 1 & \text{if } \hat{p}^{\text{crash}}_t < 0.05, \\ 0 & \text{otherwise.} \end{cases}
\end{equation}
The threshold 0.05 is chosen a priori as half of the training baseline crash rate of 0.10, so
it admits no degrees of freedom tuned on out-of-sample data.
Figure~\ref{fig:pcrash} plots the filtered crash probability over the out-of-sample window
and highlights when the shield is active. The spikes align cleanly with known crisis episodes
in 1974, 1987, 1998, 2008, 2020, and 2022.

\begin{figure}[H]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.95\textwidth]{figures/13_approach_b_pcrash_timeseries.png}
\caption{Approach B filtered crash probability, 1960 through 2026. The shield is active when
the line rises above the 0.05 threshold (horizontal dashed line), in which case the strategy
holds zero exposure that month.}
\label{fig:pcrash}
\end{figure}

Approach B's Sharpe ratio is 0.63 with 95 percent confidence interval $[0.39,\,0.86]$. The
absolute Sharpe is below DM's, but the volatility is only 8.2 percent and the maximum
drawdown is 24 percent. On a volatility-normalized basis Approach B is the most
capital-efficient of the three single approaches. It is also the only one with positive
return skew ($+0.43$), because it aggressively avoids the left tail of the UMD distribution.

\subsection{Approach C: An unsupervised two-state hidden Markov model}

Our third approach asks whether we need any supervised labels at all. A two-state Gaussian
hidden Markov model (HMM) is fit on three raw observables: the market excess return, the
six-month market realized volatility, and the six-month UMD realized variance. The model
infers regimes from the covariance structure of these observables without any reference to
whether UMD crashed next period. After fitting, we label the state with the higher in-sample
UMD mean return as the \emph{normal} regime and the other as the \emph{stressed} regime.

For each out-of-sample month we compute the filtered posterior probability that we are in
the normal regime, $P(\text{normal}_t \mid \text{obs}_{1..t})$, using the forward algorithm
with parameters learned strictly from training data. This gives a clean one-sided probability
with no backward smoothing and no use of future information. The weight rule is simply
\begin{equation}
w^{C}_t \;=\; P(\text{normal}_t \mid \text{obs}_{1..t}).
\end{equation}
The weight is continuous in $[0,\,1]$ and requires no threshold. Figure~\ref{fig:pnormal}
plots the filtered probability through time.

\begin{figure}[H]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.95\textwidth]{figures/16_approach_c_pnormal_timeseries.png}
\caption{Approach C filtered probability of the normal regime, 1960 through 2026. The HMM is
usually very confident which regime is active, with rapid transitions during crisis episodes.}
\label{fig:pnormal}
\end{figure}

Approach C's Sharpe ratio is 0.72 with 95 percent confidence interval $[0.46,\,0.96]$. The
unsupervised model essentially ties DM's 0.74 despite using no hand-crafted features, no
supervised labels, and no target returns during training. The HMM transition matrix assigns
92 to 95 percent self-persistence to each state, which matches the intuition from the macro
finance literature that market regimes are economically sticky.

\section{The \fundname{} Ensemble}

None of the three individual approaches beats the DM benchmark cleanly on its own. But they
disagree about when to be exposed through fundamentally different error mechanisms. Approach
A's errors come from L2 shrinkage attenuating DM's nonlinear interaction. Approach B's errors
come from its binary cliff: it is flat whenever the classifier is even mildly uncertain.
Approach C's errors come from inertia in the HMM posterior: it reacts to regime shifts with
a one-month lag. These error structures are decorrelated enough that averaging cancels noise
faster than it cancels signal.

Concretely, \fundname{} constructs its portfolio weight as
\begin{equation}
w^{\fundname}_t \;=\; \mathrm{clip}_{[-1,\,+3]}\!\left(\, 0.5 \cdot w^{DM}_t \;+\; 0.25 \cdot \mathbb{1}\!\{\hat{p}^{\text{crash}}_t < 0.05\} \;+\; 0.25 \cdot P(\text{normal}_t)\,\right).
\label{eq:ensemble}
\end{equation}
The 0.5, 0.25, 0.25 weighting places majority weight on DM, which remains the strongest
single linear signal, and uses Approaches B and C as complementary regime overlays. The
choice is not a knife-edge: alternative weightings near this ratio (such as 0.4, 0.3, 0.3 or
equal thirds) produce Sharpe ratios in the 0.76 to 0.80 range, so the gain is not driven by
a precisely tuned weighting scheme. Figure~\ref{fig:inddd} shows the drawdown profile.

\section{Why the Strategy Works and Why the Edge Persists}

The economic interpretation is that the momentum premium itself reflects a behavioral
mispricing. Investors under-react to news about individual firms, so information diffuses
slowly across prices, generating predictable continuation. Overlaid on this slow-diffusion
mechanism is a conditional fragility: the short leg of momentum becomes concentrated in
high-beta distressed stocks during bear markets, and when markets recover, the short leg
rebounds violently. Daniel and Moskowitz's contribution was to show that this fragility is
itself forecastable from slow-moving state variables. Our contribution is to show that
additional predictive content is extractable by methodologically diverse statistical machines,
and that an ensemble of such machines outperforms any single one out of sample.

The question of why this edge has not been arbitraged away has three components. First,
momentum timing requires net-negative exposure in a meaningful share of months (roughly 10
percent in our sample), and shorting UMD at institutional scale is capacity-constrained.
Second, most practitioner momentum funds are long-biased and benchmark explicitly to static
momentum, so active timing against that benchmark creates career risk that many managers
decline to take. Third, the timing literature itself is recent (Daniel and Moskowitz's paper
is from 2015) and the ensemble gains we document here have not yet diffused through the
practitioner community.

From a risk-management perspective, the variance decomposition
$\mathrm{Var}(R) = B\,\Sigma_F\,B^{\top} + \mathrm{Var}(U)$ from the factor-covariance
literature tells us that only 0.4 percent of \fundname's return variance is explained by its
factor loadings, and the remainder is idiosyncratic to the regime-timing logic. The strategy
does not secretly take a low-volatility or quality tilt.

The principal risks to future performance, in descending order of severity, are as follows.
First, regime-shift risk: all three components depend on similar observable inputs, so a
structural break in how crashes arrive could affect all three simultaneously. We mitigate
this through annual refits on expanding windows, so models incorporate new regimes with at
most a one-year lag. Second, short-leg capacity: in roughly 10 percent of months the ensemble
calls for a negative weight, and at institutional scale the realized cost of shorting UMD may
exceed the 20 basis point assumption. A long-only variant that clips weights at zero retains
a Sharpe of approximately 0.75, essentially tied with DM's 0.74 but without the statistical
significance gap. Third, finite out-of-sample evidence: our 66-year window is long by the
standards of quantitative research but contains a finite number of genuine regime
transitions, and extrapolation beyond this experience base is inherently uncertain.

Implementation is straightforward. At each month-end we compute the three component weights
using information available through that date, apply the ensemble formula in equation
(\ref{eq:ensemble}), clip the weight, and enter the UMD long-short position at the market
open the following day. The factor can be traded through a prime broker's systematic
long-short desk or replicated from Ken French decile sorts. Monthly portfolio turnover is
approximately 60 percent, implying roughly 700 percent annual turnover and 1.4 percent annual
cost drag.

\section{Conclusion}

Static momentum is a reliable anomaly marred by rare but severe crashes. The academic
benchmark of Daniel and Moskowitz (2015) forecasts these crashes from two slow-moving state
variables and delivers meaningful out-of-sample improvements over the static factor. Our
research shows that further improvement is available through an ensemble of three
methodologically distinct regime-detection approaches whose prediction errors are
decorrelated enough to cancel on average. The resulting \fundname{} strategy delivers a
Sharpe ratio of 0.80 with 11 percent volatility and a 22 percent maximum drawdown over the
1960 through 2026 out-of-sample window, statistically distinguishable from the DM benchmark
at the 5 percent significance level. Its monthly alpha against the Fama-French five-factor
plus momentum model is 0.33 percent ($p = 0.009$), its factor betas are near zero, and its
out-of-sample edge is robust across subsamples and to reasonable perturbations in cost
assumptions and ensemble weightings.
